The Crisis Magazine

  With its first publication in November, 1910, The Crisis Magazine preceded the start of the Harlem Renaissance. Crafted by the hand of the highly influential figure W.E.B. Dubois, the Crisis Magazine was bound for success thanks to his celebrity status and literary expertise. To no one's surprise, the Crisis was a highly politicized paper. It often hosted opinions supporting ideas of racial uplift through legal action and “The Talented Tenth”, a group of influential African-Americans who would portray their race in the brightest light possible. Various member of the Talented Tenth, like Jessie Fauset and Langston Hughes, were featured on the Crisis, and W.E.B. Dubois himself wrote many pieces for the magazine. As such, the primary focus of the paper was political.

  Even so, the Crisis had a major impact on art fields of the Harlem Renaissance. It was not uncommon to read poetry, prose, short stories, and plays in the editorial. Additionally, Dubois utilized photography as a means of adding another dimension to the Crisis. On occasion, political cartoons commenting on current events were also present. In conjunction with the work of master authors and editors, the Crisis proved to be a powerful means to inform and influence its broad audience of readers.
